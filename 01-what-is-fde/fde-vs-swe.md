# FDE vs SWE vs Other Roles

One of the most common questions candidates have: "How is FDE actually different from SWE?"

The short answer: the output is different. A SWE's output is software. An FDE's output is customer impact — delivered through software, architecture, communication, and sometimes code that runs in production.

---

## The Full Comparison

| Dimension | SWE | FDE | Solutions Engineer | Solutions Architect | TAM |
|-----------|-----|-----|-------------------|--------------------|----|
| **Primary output** | Working software | Customer success through technical solutions | Pre-sales technical win | Architecture recommendations | Relationship and adoption |
| **Customer interaction** | Rare to occasional | Frequent to constant | Frequent (pre-sales) | Project-based | Constant |
| **Coding depth** | Very deep | Deep | Light to medium | Light | Rare |
| **Product influence** | High (internal) | High (through customer feedback) | Medium | Medium | Low |
| **Ambiguity tolerance** | Medium | Very high | High | High | Medium |
| **Travel** | Minimal | Medium to high | High | Medium | High |
| **Comp ceiling** | Very high | High | Medium-high | High | Medium |
| **Interview difficulty** | Hard (DSA focus) | Hard (DSA + domain) | Medium | Medium-hard | Easy-medium |
| **AI depth required** | Role-dependent | Increasingly high | Low-medium | Medium | Low |

---

## The Key Difference in Practice

**A SWE** gets a spec, implements it, ships it.

**An FDE** gets a vague customer problem ("our recommendation system isn't converting"), figures out what's actually broken (the data pipeline is dropping 30% of events), proposes multiple solutions (quick fix vs correct fix), implements the right one under time pressure, and explains the tradeoff to the customer's VP of Engineering.

Both require excellent coding skills. Only FDE requires excellent communication skills in the same interaction.

---

## Should You Target FDE or SWE?

**Target FDE if:**
- You love solving real business problems, not just engineering problems
- You're energized by customer interaction, not drained by it
- You can context-switch between deep technical work and high-level communication
- You want to see your code in production at major companies within months of writing it

**Target SWE if:**
- You want to go deep on one product or codebase for years
- You prefer working on long-horizon projects
- Customer interaction drains you
- You want the highest possible ceiling for pure IC technical growth

**The trap:** Many engineers target FDE because they think it's "easier to get into" than pure SWE at top companies. It isn't. The bar is equally high — just in different dimensions.

---

## How FDE Compares to Solutions Engineer

Both roles talk to customers. Both roles involve technical work. The difference is *when* in the customer lifecycle.

**Solutions Engineer (SE):** Pre-sales. Showing customers what's possible. Demos, proofs of concept, technical discovery. The goal is winning the contract.

**FDE:** Post-sales. Making what was promised actually work. Production systems, deep integrations, hard problems. The goal is the customer's success (and retention).

An SE who gets very good at building the thing they're demoing often becomes an FDE. An FDE who learns to enjoy selling sometimes moves into a technical director or sales engineering lead role.

---

## How FDE Compares to Solutions Architect

**Solutions Architect (SA):** Advises on architecture. Writes diagrams. Does not typically write production code.

**FDE:** Writes the architecture *and* the code. Often is the architect and the engineer in the same room.

At companies like Google and AWS, SA is a more advisory role. FDE is more hands-on. Both can reach high levels of comp and impact — the path depends on whether you want to code or consult.

---

## How FDE Compares to Technical Account Manager

**TAM:** Relationship manager with technical background. Not typically expected to write code. Focused on adoption, risk management, and escalation.

**FDE:** Expected to write production code. The technical relationship is built through solutions, not just communication.

TAM is a valid career path but a different one. If you're reading this, you probably want FDE — the role where your technical skills have the most direct leverage.
